Affine spaces

A Notation

We will denote an element of \(\mathbf{R}\times\mathbf{R}^n\) as \((x,\mathbf{x})\) where \(x\in\mathbf{R}\) and \(\mathbf{x}\in\mathbf{R}^n\).

B Galilean structure

We will use a different notation for the basic Galilean (motion, translation, and rotation) transformations than the one used in the book in order to make the parameters more explicit:
  • Uniform motion with velocity \(\mathbf{v}\in\mathbf{R}^3\): $$U_{\mathbf{v}}(t,\mathbf{x}) = g_1(t,\mathbf{x}) = (t,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{v}t)$$
  • Translation by a vector \((s,\mathbf{s})\in\mathbf{R}\times\mathbf{R}^3\): $$T_{(s,\mathbf{s})}(t,\mathbf{x}) = g_2(t,\mathbf{x}) = (t+s,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s})$$
  • Rotation by an orthogonal matrix \(G\in\mathbf{O}_3(\mathbf{R})\): $$R_G(t,\mathbf{x}) = g_3(t,\mathbf{x}) = (t,G\mathbf{x})$$

How do uniform motion transformations behave?

\begin{align} U_{\mathbf{v+w}}(t, \mathbf{x}) & = (t, \mathbf{x}+(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})t) \\ & = (t, \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{v}t + \mathbf{w}t) \\ & = U_{\mathbf{v}}(t, \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{w}t) \\ & = U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ U_{\mathbf{w}} (t, \mathbf{x}) \end{align} which implies that \begin{align} U: \mathbf{R^3} & \rightarrow Galilean\left(\mathbf{R}\times\mathbf{R}^3\right) \\ \mathbf{v} & \mapsto U_{\mathbf{v}} \end{align} is a group homomorphism which is clearly injective. Note that \(\mathbf{R}^3\) is abelian, and therefore so is the image of \(U\); this is also clear from the calculation above.

From this we also get \(U_\mathbf{v}^{-1}=U_\mathbf{-v}\).

When looking at our Galilean space \(\mathbf{R}\times\mathbf{R}^3\) as \(\mathbf{R}^4\), if \(\mathbf{v}\in\mathbf{R}^3\) is the (column) vector \((v_1,v_2,v_3)\) then \(U_\mathbf{v}\) is represented by the matrix $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ v_1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ v_2 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ v_3 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} $$ This gives an embedding of \(\mathbf{R}^3\) in \(\mathbf{GL}_4(\mathbf{R})\). The same extends more generally to an embedding of \(\mathbf{R}^n\) in \(\mathbf{GL}_{n+1}(\mathbf{R})\).

Do basic Galilean transformations commute?

$$ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \mathbf{x}) = U_{\mathbf{v}}(t+s, \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}) = \left(t+s, \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{v}\cdot(t+s)\right) $$ whereas $$ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}(t, \mathbf{x}) = T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{v}t) = (t+s,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{v}t) $$ so that $$ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \mathbf{x}) = T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}(t, \mathbf{x}) + (0, \mathbf{v}s) $$ Thus in general \(U_{\mathbf{v}}\) and \(T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\) do not commute. They do commute, however, in the special case where \(s=0\), i.e. when there's no shift (translation) in the time dimension: $$U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})} = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}$$ (The transformations also commute when \(\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\), though this case is trivial as \(U_\mathbf{0}\) is the identity transformation).

Continuing to develop the formulae above we get: \begin{align} U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \mathbf{x}) & = \left(t+s, \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{v}\cdot(t+s)\right) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\left(t+s, \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{v}\cdot(t+s)\right) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}(t+s, \mathbf{x}) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}(t, \mathbf{x}) \end{align} and \begin{align} T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}(t, \mathbf{x}) & = (t+s,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{v}t) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}(t,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{v}t) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}(t,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}(t,\mathbf{x}) \end{align} Thus the following relations hold: \begin{align} U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})} & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{0})} \\ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}} & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\mathbf{v}}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})} \end{align}

All Galilean structures are isomorphic

Let \((X,\mathbf{R}^4,\phi)\) and \((Y,\mathbf{R}^4,\psi)\) be two Galilean structures. Pick any two point \(x_0\in X\) and \(y_0\in Y\). We define a map \(\chi:X\rightarrow Y\) as follows:
  • \(\chi(x_0) = y_0\)
  • Given \(x\in X\) there is a unique \(v\in\mathbf{R}^4\) such that \(x=x_0+v\) (that is \(x=\phi_v(x_0)\)), and we define \(\chi(x) = y_0+v\) (i.e. \(\chi(x)=\psi_v(y_0)\)). Note that for \(x=x_0\) this agrees with the definition above, that is \(v=0\) and therefore \(\chi(x_0) = y_0\).
The map \(\chi\) is an isomorphism of Galilean structures. Indeed, by construction the diagram \begin{CD} \mathbf{R}^4 @>id>> \mathbf{R}^4\\ @V \phi V V @VV \psi V\\ X @>>\chi> Y \end{CD} commutes.
