Affine spaces

A Notation

We will denote an element of \(\Gn\) as \((x,\bfx)\) where \(x\in\R\) and \(\bfx\in\Rn\).

Claim Let \(\mathbf{A}\) be an affine \(n\)-space and \(a_0,a_1,a_2\in\mathbf{A}\). Then $$(a_2-a_1) = (a_2-a_0)-(a_1-a_0)$$ in \(\Rn\).

Proof - by definition \((a_1-a_0)\) is the (unique) element of \(\Rn\) such that \(a_0+(a_1-a_0)=a_1\). Similarly \(a_0+(a_2-a_0)=a_2\) and \(a_1+(a_2-a_1)=a_2\). We therefore get \begin{align} a_0+\left((a_2-a_1)+(a_1-a_0)\right) & = a_0+\left((a_1-a_0)+(a_2-a_1)\right) \\ & = \left(a_0+(a_1-a_0)\right)+(a_2-a_1) \\ & = a_1+(a_2-a_1) \\ & = a_2 \end{align} so by uniqueness we get \(a_2-a_0=(a_2-a_1)+(a_1-a_0)\). This gives the desired result.

B Galilean structure

We will use a different notation for the basic Galilean (motion, translation, and rotation) transformations than the one used in the book in order to make the parameters more explicit:
  • Uniform motion with velocity \(\bfv\in\Rthree\): $$U_{\bfv}(t,\bfx) = g_1(t,\bfx) = (t,\bfx+\bfv t)$$
  • Translation by a vector \((s,\mathbf{s})\in\G\): $$T_{(s,\mathbf{s})}(t,\bfx) = g_2(t,\bfx) = (t+s,\bfx+\mathbf{s})$$
  • Rotation by an orthogonal matrix \(G\in\mathbf{O}_3(\R)\): $$R_G(t,\bfx) = g_3(t,\bfx) = (t,G\bfx)$$

Basic Galilean transformations

Uniform motion Galilean transformations
\begin{align} U_{\mathbf{v+w}}(t, \bfx) & = (t, \bfx+(\bfv+\mathbf{w})t) \\ & = (t, \bfx + \bfv t + \mathbf{w}t) \\ & = U_{\bfv}(t, \bfx + \mathbf{w}t) \\ & = U_{\bfv}\circ U_{\mathbf{w}} (t, \bfx) \end{align} which implies that \begin{align} U: \Rthree & \rightarrow Galilean\left(\G\right) \\ \bfv & \mapsto U_{\bfv} \end{align} is a group homomorphism which is clearly injective. Note that \(\Rthree\) is abelian, and therefore so is the image of \(U\); this is also clear from the calculation above.

From this we also get \(U_\bfv^{-1}=U_\mathbf{-v}\).

When looking at our Galilean space \(\G\) as \(\Rto{4}\), if \(\bfv\in\Rthree\) is the (column) vector \((v_1,v_2,v_3)\) then \(U_\bfv\) is represented by the matrix $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ v_1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ v_2 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ v_3 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} $$ This gives an embedding of \(\Rthree\) in \(\mathbf{GL}_4(\R)\). The same extends more generally to an embedding of \(\Rn\) in \(\mathbf{GL}_{n+1}(\R)\).

Translation Galilean transformations
Clearly \(T_{(s+s',\mathbf{s}+\mathbf{s'})} = T_{(s,\mathbf{s})}+T_{(s',\mathbf{s'})}\) and \(T_{(s,\mathbf{s})}^{-1}=T_{(-s,\mathbf{-s})}\). In particular the map \(T:(s,\mathbf{s})\mapsto T_(s,\mathbf{s})\) is a group homomorphism, in fact a monomorphism, embedding \(\G\) in \(Galilean\left(\G\right)\).

Note, however, that with the exception of \((s,\mathbf{s})=(0,\mathbf{0})\), \(T_(s,\mathbf{s})\) is not a linear transformation as it does not preserve the origin.

Rotation Galilean transformations
Do uniform motion and translation transformations commute?
$$ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \bfx) = U_{\bfv}(t+s, \bfx+\mathbf{s}) = \left(t+s, \bfx+\mathbf{s}+\bfv\cdot(t+s)\right) $$ whereas $$ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}(t, \bfx) = T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t,\bfx+\bfv t) = (t+s,\bfx+\mathbf{s}+\bfv t) $$ so that $$ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \bfx) = T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}(t, \bfx) + (0, \bfv s) $$ Thus in general \(U_{\bfv}\) and \(T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\) do not commute. They do commute, however, in the special case where \(s=0\), i.e. when there's no shift (translation) in the time dimension: $$U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})} = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}$$ (The transformations also commute when \(\bfv=\mathbf{0}\), though this case is trivial as \(U_\mathbf{0}\) is the identity transformation).

Continuing to develop the formulae above we get: \begin{align} U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \bfx) & = \left(t+s, \bfx+\mathbf{s}+\bfv\cdot(t+s)\right) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\left(t+s, \bfx+\bfv\cdot(t+s)\right) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}(t+s, \bfx) \\ & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}(t, \bfx) \end{align} and \begin{align} T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}(t, \bfx) & = (t+s,\bfx+\mathbf{s}+\bfv t) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}(t,\bfx+\mathbf{s}+\bfv t) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\bfv}(t,\bfx+\mathbf{s}) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}(t,\bfx) \end{align} Thus the following relations hold: \begin{align} U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})} & = T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{0})} \\ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}\circ U_{\bfv} & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\circ U_{\bfv}\circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})} \end{align}

Do translation and rotation transformations commute?
\begin{align} R_G\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{s})}(t, \bfx) & = R_G(t+s, \bfx+\mathbf{s}) \\ & = \left(t+s, G(\bfx+\mathbf{s})\right) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}\left(t, G(\bfx+\mathbf{s})\right) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})} \circ R_G(t, \bfx+\mathbf{s}) \\ & = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})} \circ R_G \circ T_{(0, \mathbf{s})}(t, \bfx) \end{align} Note that time-translation and rotation transformations do commute: $$R_G\circ T_{(s, \mathbf{0})}(t, \bfx) = T_{(s, \mathbf{0})} \circ R_G(t, \bfx)$$ This is expected because time-translations operate on the \(\R\) part of \(\G\) whereas rotations operate on the \(\Rthree\) part of \(\G\).

PROBLEM. All Galilean structures are isomorphic

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be two Galilean structures. Pick any two point \(x_0\in X\) and \(y_0\in Y\). We define a map \(\chi:X\rightarrow Y\) as follows:
  • \(\chi(x_0) = y_0\)
  • Given \(x\in X\) there is a unique \(v\in\Rto{4}\) such that \(x=x_0+v\), and we define \(\chi(x) = y_0+v\). Note that for \(x=x_0\) this agrees with the definition above, that is \(v=0\) and therefore \(\chi(x_0) = y_0\).
The map \(\chi\) is an isomorphism of Galilean structures. Indeed, by definition of affine spaces \(\chi\) is one-to-one and by construction satisfies \(\chi(x)-\chi(x_0)=x-x_0\). It follows then that \(\chi(x')-\chi(x)=x'-x\) for every \(x,x'\in\mathbf{A}\), so \(\chi\) preserves the Galilean structure.

If \(\phi\) is a Galilean transformation on \(\G\) then \(\phi\) extends naturally to a transformation on \(\GN\), which we also denote by \(\phi\). Indeed, consider the following diagram: \begin{CD} \GN @. \GN\\ @VVV @VVV\\ \left(\G\right)^n @>\phi^n>> \left(\G\right)^n \end{CD} where the vertical arrows send \((t,\mathbf{x_1}, \dots, \mathbf{x_n})\) to \(\left((t,\mathbf{x_1}), \dots, (t,\mathbf{x_n})\right)\).

D Newton's equations

E Constraints imposed by the principle of relativity

Example 1

Assume that \(\bfx=\varphi(t)\) is a solution of equation (1) and denote by \(\varphi_s\) the function mapping \(t\mapsto \varphi(t+s)\). Then, as discussed in the book, \(\bfx=\varphi_s(t)\) is a solution of equation (1) as well, that is $$\ddot{\varphi}_s(t)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}_s(t), \varphi_s(t), t\right).$$ From the chain rule we get \(\dot{\varphi}_s(t)=\dot{\varphi}(t+s)\) and \(\ddot{\varphi}_s(t)=\ddot{\varphi}(t+s)\). Plugging these identities into the differential equation satisfied by \(\varphi_s\) above we get: $$\ddot{\varphi}(t+s)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}(t+s), \varphi(t+s), t\right).$$ Changing variable \(T=t+s\) and using the chain rule this transforms into $$\ddot{\varphi}(T)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}(T), \varphi(T), T-s\right).$$ But \(\varphi(T)\) is also a solution of the differential equation, that is, $$\ddot{\varphi}(T)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}(T), \varphi(T), T\right),$$ and combining these two equations we see that $$\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}(T), \varphi(T), T\right)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\varphi}(T), \varphi(T), T-s\right).$$ Now let \(\bfx_0,\dot{\bfx}_0\in\RN\) be two points of the form \(\bfx_0=\varphi(t_0),\dot{\bfx}_0=\dot{\varphi}(t_0)\) where \(\varphi\) is a solution of equation (1). Then for any number \(s\in\R\) we get: $$\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\bfx}_0, \bfx_0, t_0\right)=\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\bfx}_0, \bfx_0, t_0-s\right),$$ which, from the definition of the derivative, implies that $$\left.\frac{d\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\bfx}_0, \bfx_0, T\right)}{dT}\right|_{T=t_0}=0.$$ Finally, from the theory of differential equations we know that for any triplet \((\dot{\bfx}_0, \bfx_0, t_0)\in\RN\times\RN\times\R\) there is a solution \(\varphi\) of equation (1) which satisfies \(\bfx_0=\varphi(t_0),\dot{\bfx}_0=\dot{\varphi}(t_0)\), and it follows that $$\left.\frac{d\mathbf{F}\left(\dot{\bfx}, \bfx, T\right)}{dT}\right|_{T=t}=0$$ for every value of \(t\). That is, \(F\) does not depend on \(T\).

Example 2

We start by looking at translations in 3d space. For simplicity we will denote an \(N\)-vector \((\bfx_1,\dots,\bfx_n)\in\RN\) by \(\angular{\bfx_i}\). Recall that each \(\bfx_i\) is a 3-vector, that is \(\bfx_i\in\Rthree\) and \(N=3n\).

Let \(\bfx_i=\varphi_i(t)\) (\(i=1, 2, \dots, n)\) be a solution for equation (1). Let \(\bfr\in\Rthree\) be any translation vector and define \(\psi_i(t)=\varphi_i(t)+\bfr\). Then, as discussed in the text, \(\varphi_i(t)+\bfr\) (\(i=1, 2, \dots, n)\) is also a solution for (1). That is, $$ \angular{\ddot{\varphi_i}(t)}=\mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)}, \angular{\varphi_i(t)}, t\right) $$ and $$ \angular{\ddot{\psi_i}(t)}=\mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\psi_i}(t)}, \angular{\psi_i(t)}, t\right). $$ But \(\dot{\psi_i}=\dot{\varphi_i}\) and \(\ddot{\psi_i}=\ddot{\varphi_i}\), so we get $$ \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)}, \angular{\varphi_i(t)+\bfr}, t\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)}, \angular{\varphi_i(t)}, t\right). $$ Now for every points \(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}\in\RN\) and \(t_0\in\R\) there's a solution \(\angular{\varphi_i}\) of equation (1) such that \(\varphi_i(t_0)=\bfx_i\) and \(\dot{\varphi_i}(t_0)=\dot{\bfx}_i\) for every \(i\). Combining these two results we get $$ \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i+\bfr}, t\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}, t\right) \tag{*} $$ for every \(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}\in\RN\), \(t_0\in\R\), and \(\bfr\in\Rthree\).

Change coordinates as follows: $$ \begin{aligned} \bfy_1 &= \bfx_1+\bfx_2+\cdots+\bfx_n,\\ \bfy_i &= \bfx_i-\bfx_1\ \ \ \text{for}\ i=2, 3, \dots, n, \end{aligned} $$ keeping the \(\dot{\bfx}_i\)'s and \(t\) intact. This indeed has an inverse: $$ \begin{aligned} \bfx_1 & =\frac{1}{n}\left(\bfy_1-\sum_{i=2}^{n}\bfy_i\right)\\ \bfx_i &= \bfy_i+\bfx_1\ \ \ \text{for}\ i=2, 3, \dots, n\text{.} \end{aligned} $$ Now define \(\mathbf{G}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfy_i}, t\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}, t\right)\) and let \(f_j\), \(g_j\) denote the \(j\)'th coordinate of \(\mathbf{F}\), \(\mathbf{G}\), respectively, for \(j=1,2,\dots,N\). Let \(\mathbf{h}\in\Rthree\) be any vector and define \(\mathbf{r}=\frac{1}{n}\mathbf{h}\). Then using (*) we get $$ \begin{aligned} g_j\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \bfy_1+\mathbf{h}, \angular{\bfy_i}_{i=2}^{n}, t\right) & = f_j\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i+\mathbf{r}}, t\right)\\ & = f_j\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}, t\right)\\ & = g_j\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfy_i}, t\right). \end{aligned} $$ It follows, then, from the definition of derivative (applied to each one of the 3 coordinates of \(\bfy_1\) separately) that $$\frac{\partial g_j}{\partial\bfy_1}=0$$ (as a 3-vector). This shows that \(\mathbf{G}\) does not depend on \(\bfy_1\), which in turn implies that \(\mathbf{F}\) depends only on the "relative coordinates" \(\{\bfx_k-\bfx_l\}.\) This proves the first part of the example.

We turn now to the second part of the example, namely to the case of a uniform motion with velocity \(\bfv\in\Rthree\). Let \(\bfx_i=\varphi_i(t)\) (\(i=1, 2, \dots, n)\) be a solution for equation (1): $$ \angular{\ddot{\varphi_i}(t)}=\mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)}, \{\varphi_i(t)-\varphi_j(t)\}\right). $$ Here we used the previous results. Let \(\bfv\in\Rthree\) be any uniform motion vector and define \(\psi_i(t)=\varphi_i(t)+\bfv t\) for \(i=1, 2, \dots, n\). Then \(\{\psi_i(t)\}\) is also a solution for (1): $$ \angular{\ddot{\psi_i}(t)}=\mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\psi_i}(t)}, \{\psi_i(t)-\psi_j(t)\}\right). $$ Since \(\psi_i(t)-\psi_j(t)=\varphi_i(t)-\varphi_j(t)\), \(\dot{\psi_i}=\dot{\varphi_i}+\bfv\), and \(\ddot{\psi_i}=\ddot{\varphi_i}\) we get $$ \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)+\bfv}, \{\varphi_i(t)-\varphi_j(t)\}\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\varphi_i}(t)}, \{\varphi_i(t)-\varphi_j(t)\}\right), $$ and as before the theory of ordinary differential equations shows that $$ \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i+\bfv}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right) $$ for every \(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \angular{\bfx_i}\in\RN\) and \(\bfv\in\Rthree\). We follow a line of reasoning similar to that of the first part above applied to the following change of coordinates: $$ \begin{aligned} \dot{\bfy}_1 &= \dot{\bfx}_1+\dot{\bfx}_2+\cdots+\dot{\bfx}_n,\\ \dot{\bfy}_i &= \dot{\bfx}_i-\dot{\bfx}_1\ \ \ \text{for}\ i=2, 3, \dots, n, \end{aligned} $$ The \(\bfx_i\)'s are left unchanged. Define \(\mathbf{G}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfy}_i}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right)= \mathbf{F}\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right)\) and let \(f_k\), \(g_k\), be the \(k\)'th coordinate of \(\mathbf{F}\), \(\mathbf{G},\) respectively, \(\mathbf{h}\in\Rthree\), and \(\mathbf{r}=\frac{1}{n}\mathbf{h}\) be as in the first part. Then $$ \begin{aligned} g_k\left(\dot{\bfy}_1+\mathbf{h}, \angular{\dot{\bfy}_i}_{i=2}^{n}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right) & = f_k\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i+\mathbf{r}}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right)\\ & = f_k\left(\angular{\dot{\bfx}_i}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right)\\ & = g_k\left(\angular{\dot{\bfy}_i}, \{\bfx_i-\bfx_j\}\right). \end{aligned} $$ Thus when calculating the 3-vector derivative of \(g_k\) with respect to \(\dot{\bfy}_1\) we get $$\frac{\partial g_j}{\partial\dot{\bfy}_1}=0.$$ So \(\mathbf{G}\) does not depend on \(\dot{\bfy}_1\), and therefore \(\mathbf{F}\) depends only on the "relative coordinates" \(\{\dot{\bfx}_k-\dot{\bfx}_l\}.\) This concludes the proof of example 2.
